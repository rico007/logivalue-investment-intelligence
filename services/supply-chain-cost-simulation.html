Supply Chain Cost Simulation for Logistics Real Estate

By Raimund Paetzmann and Carl-Friedrich zu Knyphausen · Logivalue GmbH · Last updated April 2026

Key Takeaways

  • Supply chain cost simulation models occupier willingness-to-pay based on total logistics costs — not headline rent comparisons
  • The methodology uses 42 site variables and 65 scoring profiles to evaluate each property against multiple tenant types
  • It identifies which tenants will pay premium rents and commit to long-term leases for a specific building
  • The spread between best-case and worst-case tenant for a single building can exceed 30%

What Is Supply Chain Cost Simulation?

Supply chain cost simulation is a valuation methodology that models how a building's physical characteristics, location, and operational capabilities affect a tenant's total logistics costs. Instead of comparing headline rents of similar buildings, it determines what each tenant type would actually pay — based on the building's economic value within their supply chain.

This approach produces more accurate valuations because it captures the operational reality that drives occupier decisions. A building that reduces a tenant's transport costs by 8% is worth significantly more to that tenant than a comparable building that does not — regardless of what nearby properties are charging. By modelling these economics, investors can identify the use cases that yield premium rents and long-term commitment.

"When you understand a tenant's total supply chain cost — transport, labour, automation, throughput — you can identify which buildings will command premium rents and which tenants will stay for the long term. That's the foundation of stable cashflows."

— Raimund Paetzmann, Occupier-Side Strategist, Logivalue

How Does the Simulation Work?

The simulation evaluates each property against two comprehensive datasets: 42 site variables that describe the building and its environment, and 65 scoring profiles that represent different occupier types and their operational requirements.

42 Site Variables

The site variables span four categories that capture everything a logistics occupier considers when evaluating a building:

  • Location variables — motorway access time, distance to major population centres, proximity to port and rail terminals, local congestion patterns, time-to-customer for key delivery windows
  • Building specification variables — clear height, floor loading capacity, dock-door count and configuration, column spacing, expansion potential, power supply capacity, automation readiness
  • Operational variables — throughput capacity per shift, temperature control capability, cross-dock feasibility, hazardous goods compliance, yard depth and trailer staging capacity
  • Market variables — local labour pool size and wage rates, competing supply within catchment, permitting constraints, zoning stability, tenant demand by type

65 Scoring Profiles

Each scoring profile represents a distinct occupier type — from high-frequency parcel sortation to pharmaceutical cold chain, from fashion e-commerce returns processing to grocery micro-fulfilment. The profiles encode how each occupier type weighs the 42 site variables, reflecting the operational priorities that drive their location and building decisions.

For example, a parcel carrier's scoring profile weights dock-door count, motorway access time, and yard capacity heavily, while assigning lower importance to clear height and automation readiness. A grocery micro-fulfilment operator's profile inverts these priorities, weighting power supply, clear height, and urban proximity above dock-door configuration. These profiles are grounded in the operational experience of Logivalue's team, who have collectively delivered 40+ fulfilment centres across Europe for operators including Amazon, Zalando, and DHL.

Why Does This Produce Better Valuations?

Traditional comparables answer one question: what are similar buildings renting for? Supply chain cost simulation answers a different, more valuable question: which tenant types would pay the most for this specific building, and why?

The difference matters because two buildings at the same headline rent can have vastly different value to different occupier types. A cross-dock operator on razor-thin margins values a building's dock-door configuration and yard depth. A last-mile operator values its proximity to consumers. A cold chain tenant values its power supply and insulation potential. By modelling these economics, the simulation identifies the use case that unlocks the highest rent — often 15-30% above the comparable-based estimate.

"Most logistics valuations compare the building to other buildings. We compare the building to what it does for the tenant's supply chain. That tells you what the tenant will actually pay — and whether they'll stay."

— Carl-Friedrich zu Knyphausen, Managing Director, Logivalue

What the Simulation Reveals

A completed supply chain cost simulation produces several outputs that traditional valuation cannot:

  • Ranked tenant types — which occupier profiles would extract the most value from this building, ordered by willingness-to-pay
  • Optimal use case — the specific use case that maximises rental income and lease duration
  • Rent sensitivity analysis — how changes in building specification (additional dock doors, higher power supply) affect achievable rent
  • Tenant stability assessment — which use cases create the deepest operational dependency, reducing re-letting risk
  • CAPEX-to-rent ratio — whether building modifications to attract a higher-value tenant justify the investment

These outputs give investors a decision-grade view of a property's income potential — grounded in operational reality rather than market generalisation.

When Supply Chain Cost Simulation Adds the Most Value

The simulation is particularly valuable in three scenarios where traditional comparables fall short:

  • Repositioning opportunities — buildings where a change in property type or tenant type could unlock significantly higher rents. The simulation quantifies exactly what that uplift is worth
  • Specialised assets — cold chain, automation-ready, or high-security facilities where comparable transactions are scarce and tenant economics are complex
  • Portfolio optimisation — when evaluating multiple assets across a portfolio, the simulation identifies which properties are underletting relative to their operational potential
